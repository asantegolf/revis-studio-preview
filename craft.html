A method that has not been improved upon in five hundred years.

Each Revis carpet begins with wool shorn from sheep on the high plateaus of the Himalaya, and ends with a hand wash in spring water before it is rolled and shipped. In between is a method that has been refined — but never improved upon — for half a millennium.

In short

A Revis carpet is hand‑knotted in Kathmandu from Tibetan long‑staple wool, batch‑dyed with vegetable pigments or Swiss synthetic dyes, finished in spring water, and shipped to your room. A 9 × 12 takes six to nine months on the loom.

I Wool

Himalayan long‑staple

Our base fibre is the wool of sheep raised on the high plateaus of the Himalaya, at altitudes between 3,000 and 4,500 metres. At elevation, the sheep grow a long, springy, exceptionally lanolin‑rich staple. Lanolin is the natural oil that gives wool its sheen, its soil resistance, and the slow patina that distinguishes an heirloom from a new rug.

The wool is hand‑sheared, sorted by hand, and hand‑carded and hand‑spun into yarn in the foothills of Nepal before it is ever sent to the dye lab. No carbonising. No chemical bleaching. The fibre arrives at the loom alive.

II Silk & allo

Highlights, by request

Where a design needs a higher‑contrast highlight — or simply more light — we blend in hand‑reeled Chinese mulberry silk, or allo, the Himalayan nettle fibre that produces a long, cool, almost flax‑like sheen.

Silk and allo are knotted into the design rather than woven separately, which lets a Revis carpet glow without ever looking glossy.

III Dye

Madder, indigo, walnut, pomegranate

Color is the most personal decision in a carpet. Every Revis project is sampled against the actual light of the room — north light is not east light, and a New York afternoon is not a Hamptons noon.

Where we can, we use traditional vegetable pigments: madder root for the warm reds, true indigo for the blues, walnut and pomegranate for the umbers and ochres. Where a project will live under a south‑facing skylight or in a hotel lobby, we switch to premium Swiss synthetic dyes rated for ultraviolet stability — the same class used in heritage carpet restoration.

Dye lots are tied to the project. We do not pool yarn between commissions.

IV The knot

Hand‑knotted, the Tibetan way.

The Tibetan knot — also called the senna or cut‑loop knot — is tied around a rod the width of the desired pile. When the row is complete, the rod is slid out, and a horizontal cut down the row releases each loop into two clean ends.

The advantage is precision: the pile is cut to a single, exact height across the whole carpet. It is also the only knot that allows the high‑contrast graphic edges that contemporary architecture asks for, without ever feeling printed.

A skilled weaver ties between 8,000 and 10,000 knots a day. A 9 × 12 carpet at 100 knots per square inch contains roughly 1.5 million knots. Six months on the loom is normal. Nine is not unusual.

V Finish

Spring‑water wash, sun stretch

After knotting, the rug is washed in cold spring water to set the dye and bloom the natural lanolin. It is then sun‑stretched on a clean stone bed — never on a machine — to lock the geometry of the field.

Where the design calls for it, we hand‑carve the pile to relieve edges, soften gradients and bring out the drawing. The finished carpet is rolled by hand, wrapped in unbleached muslin, and shipped to the room it was drawn for.

Glossary

A few terms, plainly

Tibetan knot
A cut‑loop knot tied around a rod, then released by a single horizontal cut. Yields a perfectly uniform pile. Used by Revis on every carpet.
Cartoon
A full‑scale technical drawing of a carpet, hand‑coloured to spec, used by the weaver as the literal map of the rug.
Abrash
The subtle variation in tone across a single field of color, the natural fingerprint of a small dye lot. A virtue, not a defect.
Allo
A Himalayan nettle fibre with a long, cool sheen. Sometimes blended into a Revis carpet where extra light is wanted.
Lanolin
The natural oil in wool. Soil‑repelling, light‑catching. The reason a good wool rug looks better with use.
